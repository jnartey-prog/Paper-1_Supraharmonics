Title: Analytical Framework for Supraharmonic Aggregation Under Spatial Uncertainty Using Point Process Theory

Abstract


Keywords
1. Introduction
1.1 Supraharmonics (2‚Äď150 kHz) as an Emerging Power-Quality Problem
The accelerating deployment of switched-mode power supplies, grid-connected inverters, electric vehicle (EV) chargers, battery energy storage systems, and LED lighting drivers is fundamentally reshaping the electromagnetic environment of low-voltage (LV) distribution networks. Unlike conventional power-electronic loads, which introduce distortion predominantly within the classical harmonic range (integer multiples of the 50/60 Hz fundamental up to approximately 2 kHz), modern converter-interfaced devices emit conducted voltage and current disturbances at substantially higher frequencies. These components, occupying the interval from 2 kHz to 150 kHz, are now widely designated in the power-quality literature as supraharmonics (SH), a term that demarcates them from classical harmonics below and radiated electromagnetic interference (EMI) bands above 150 kHz (Alfalahi et al., 2021; De Falco & Varilone, 2021). The switching-related and wideband nature of these emissions distinguishes them from integer-multiple harmonic distortion and necessitates distinct analytical treatment (Peiris et al., 2025; Sandrolini & Mariscotti, 2021).
The lower boundary of the supraharmonic band at 2 kHz marks the practical transition point beyond which classical harmonic analysis methods, typically based on discrete Fourier transform (DFT) windows synchronised to the power frequency, incur significant accuracy penalties (Li et al., 2023; Sandrolini & Mariscotti, 2021). The upper boundary at 150 kHz aligns with conducted-emission regulatory practice and reflects the widely adopted demarcation between conducted power-quality phenomena and radiated EMI (Lodetti et al., 2023; Mariscotti & Mingotti, 2024). Within this band, intentional power-line communication (PLC) signals coexist with switching-related emissions, creating a regime in which unintentional distortion and deliberate communication carriers may interact (Mariscotti & Mingotti, 2024; Wasowski et al., 2021).
The grid-level consequences of supraharmonic distortion arise from the interaction between device emissions and frequency-selective network impedance. Resonance conditions shaped by feeder topology can amplify SH voltage levels beyond what individual source emissions would suggest in isolation (Letha et al., 2021; Sutaria & R√∂nnberg, 2023). Frequency-dependent losses in transformers, cables, and insulation are linked to elevated SH content (Mariscotti & Mingotti, 2024). Bidirectional EV charging stations modify network impedance at supraharmonic frequencies, introducing additional resonance points (Grasel et al., 2022). Measurement uncertainty also increases above 10 kHz due to instrument transformer and meter limitations (Rom et al., 2025).
Feeder-resolved EMT and frequency-domain propagation models remain indispensable when topology, impedance spectra, and injection locations are well characterised and the objective is accurate site-specific prediction. However, aggregate SH distortion at the point of common coupling (PCC) depends not only on device-level emission but on the interaction of spatially distributed, randomly active sources with a frequency-selective network. When spatial deployment and activity patterns are only partially observed, deterministic feeder-specific models do not directly yield generalisable scaling laws or uncertainty-dependent screening metrics across deployment scenarios. This motivates complementary theoretical formulations that characterise aggregation mechanisms under idealized but analytically tractable conditions.
1.2 EV Chargers and Converter-Interfaced Loads as Dominant Stochastic Sources
Among converter-interfaced technologies, EV supply equipment (EVSE) and grid-connected PV inverters are consistently documented contributors to SH emissions in the 2-150 kHz band. Field measurements show both narrowband and wideband components and demonstrate that single high-power chargers can modify local high-frequency network impedance (Grasel et al., 2022). Multi-unit installations exhibit sub-linear aggregation due to diversity and impedance-absorption effects (Slangen et al., 2023). PV-connected feeders show resonance-driven amplification under varying operating conditions (Letha et al., 2021; Singh et al., 2023).
Crucially, emission characteristics vary with operating state, charging stage, and background conditions (Giri & R√∂nnberg, 2025; Slangen et al., 2023; Sutaria & R√∂nnberg, 2023). Consequently, both the number of simultaneously active sources and their effective injection characteristics are inherently stochastic. This variability suggests that aggregate SH distortion is not simply a deterministic function of installed capacity but a statistical outcome shaped by activity, phase dispersion, and network propagation.
1.3 Why Supraharmonic Aggregation Is Inherently Statistical
Aggregate PCC voltage is not the arithmetic sum of individual spectra but the result of stochastic phasor superposition weighted by frequency-dependent transfer impedance. Independent converter switching leads to phase asynchrony, shifting aggregate scaling from linear summation toward root-sum-square behaviour (Mariscotti et al., 2022). Field measurements confirm sub-linear aggregation under diversity (Slangen et al., 2023).
Propagation is frequency selective; resonance peaks amplify emissions depending on spatial location (Letha et al., 2021). Thus, aggregate distortion reflects a spatially weighted sum of random phasors. Deterministic impedance modelling captures these mechanisms for known feeders but does not directly provide density-dependent statistical scaling or tail-risk metrics under deployment uncertainty.
Existing aggregation approaches primarily rely on (i) feeder-specific EMT simulations, (ii) Monte Carlo phase randomisation with deterministic source locations, or (iii) empirical diversity factors. These approaches provide important evidence of sub-linear summation and resonance sensitivity, but they typically do not yield closed-form density-dependent statistics under explicit spatial uncertainty, nor do they provide tractable analytical inversion for screening purposes. This motivates investigation of analytically tractable spatial-uncertainty formulations.
1.4 Spatial Uncertainty and Limitations of Feeder-Specific Simulations for Generalisation
Spatial uncertainty encompasses unknown device positions, uncertain simultaneous activity, variable deployment density, and topology variability. Even feeders with similar nominal parameters exhibit distinct resonance amplification profiles (Letha et al., 2021). Aggregation outcomes depend on spatial separation, not just source count (Slangen et al., 2023).
Feeder-specific EMT models are the appropriate tools when detailed topology and impedance data are available. However, when planning under incomplete spatial information‚ÄĒsuch as transformer-level hosting capacity screening‚ÄĒthere is a need for models that explicitly encode spatial randomness rather than fixing source locations deterministically. This motivates consideration of spatial point-process formulations.
1.5 Theoretical Framework and Contributions
This work develops a stochastic-geometry framework for analysing supraharmonic aggregation mechanisms under spatial uncertainty and electrical diversity. The analysis adopts four core assumptions to enable analytical tractability: (i) independent phase angles across chargers (phase asynchrony), (ii) homogeneous Poisson spatial distribution of charger locations, (iii) linear network propagation, and (iv) quasi-stationary deployment statistics. The framework yields closed-form expressions for aggregate voltage moments, semi-analytical characterizations of high-percentile and exceedance-probability metrics, and analytical scaling laws relating charger density to RMS and percentile distortion levels.
The paper addresses three mechanism questions under the stated assumptions: (1) What density-scaling relationship governs RMS voltage under phase asynchrony, and how does this scaling change under partial phase coherence? (2) Under what kernel regularity and mark-moment conditions do aggregate statistics remain well-defined? (3) Can tail metrics and exceedance probabilities be constructed semi-analytically in a manner consistent with Monte Carlo simulations? These questions are evaluated through frequency-resolved comparison between analytical predictions and Monte Carlo realisations generated under identical model assumptions. The results reveal ‚ąöőĽ density scaling under phase diversity, coherence-driven transition toward linear scaling, and robustness of analytical tail approximations to frequency and spatial heterogeneity. Because the objective of the study is analytical mechanism analysis rather than empirical prediction, validation in this work refers to internal verification of analytical expressions against Monte Carlo realisations generated under identical model assumptions; empirical validation against field measurements is identified as future work.
1.6 Scope and Limitations
This framework is designed for population-level statistical screening under limited spatial knowledge rather than site-specific compliance prediction. The stated assumptions‚ÄĒphase independence, spatial homogeneity, linear propagation, and quasi-stationarity‚ÄĒrepresent idealized conditions. Real networks may exhibit phase correlation due to synchronized control, spatial clustering due to infrastructure siting, nonlinear resonances, and time-varying charger activity. Empirical validation of assumption validity in real distribution systems and characterization of how departures from these assumptions influence aggregation behaviour are identified as critical future work. Empirical measurements quantifying phase-angle correlation across multi-charger EV installations remain limited in the literature; phase independence is therefore adopted as a tractable baseline assumption, with coherence sensitivity analysed parametrically via the correlation factor ŌĀ.

2.2 Network‚ÄďSource Abstraction and Structural Assumptions
The analytical framework is constructed upon a hierarchy of abstractions that render spatial aggregation mathematically tractable while preserving physical interpretability. This section formalises the structural assumptions under which the supraharmonic aggregation problem admits well-defined statistical characterisation.

Assumption 1 (Linear Time-Invariant Propagation)
Network propagation is modelled as linear and time-invariant within each frequency bin of the supraharmonic band. For a fixed frequency f, the mapping between injected current and PCC voltage is deterministic and governed by a transfer impedance Z_tr (f,d).
No cross-frequency coupling is considered; the aggregation problem decomposes into independent frequency-indexed scalar problems. Time-varying impedance effects, nonlinear network interactions, and modulation-induced inter-frequency products are outside the present scope.

Assumption 2 (Quasi-Stationary Deployment and Emission Statistics)
Spatial deployment intensity and electrical mark distributions are treated as stationary over the aggregation window. That is, the spatial point process and associated mark statistics do not vary over the time scale relevant to frequency-domain evaluation.
This assumption permits ensemble-based statistical characterisation without modelling slow temporal drift in charger activity or network conditions.

Assumption 3 (Finite Mark Moments)
Electrical marks m_i (f)=(I_i (f),Y_i (f))satisfy finite second-moment conditions at each frequency:
E[‚ą£I_i (f)‚ą£^2]<‚ąě,E[‚ą£Y_i (f)‚ą£^2]<‚ąě.

These conditions ensure existence of the first and second moments of the aggregate voltage, subject to kernel integrability (Assumption A5). Heavy-tailed emission models violating finite-moment conditions are not considered in the baseline formulation.

Assumption 4 (Baseline Phase Independence)
Unless otherwise specified, phase angles of injected currents are treated as independent across sources. Under uniform phase diversity, E[V(f)]=0, and aggregation is governed by second-order statistics.
Departures from independence (e.g., partial or full phase coherence) are treated explicitly as alternative regimes. In such cases, non-zero mean contributions emerge and scaling behaviour may change accordingly.

Assumption 5 (Kernel Regularity and Integrability)
The transfer impedance Z_tr (f,d)satisfies:
	Determinism: For fixed fand d, the kernel is deterministic.
	Measurability and continuity: The mapping d‚Ü¶Z_tr (f,d)is measurable and piecewise continuous.
	Bounded effective response: The modified kernel
(Z_tr (f,d))/(1+Y_i (f)Z_tr (f,d))

remains bounded over the spatial domain of interest.
	Spatial integrability: Over bounded service regions, kernel magnitude is bounded. In asymptotic infinite-domain thought experiments, sufficient spatial decay (e.g., exponential or sufficiently fast power-law attenuation) ensures convergence of integrals governing moment existence.
These conditions guarantee that the aggregate shot-noise functional admits finite first and second moments under the stated mark assumptions.

Scope Delimitation
The framework separates three sources of uncertainty:
	spatial randomness (deployment configuration),
	electrical mark variability (source behaviour),
	deterministic network filtering (propagation kernel).
Graph-constrained deployment, clustering processes, time-varying intensity fields, and nonlinear network responses are not included in the baseline formulation, though the analytical structure permits extension to alternative spatial models.

Section 2.2, therefore, establishes the structural conditions under which the supraharmonic aggregation functional is mathematically well posed. Subsequent sections derive moment expressions, density-scaling laws, and tail metrics under these assumptions, and examine the effects of controlled departures from phase independence and from kernel attenuation regimes.

The attenuation envelope, bounded resonance gain, and denominator-separation condition are modelling controls introduced to ensure finite-moment conditions and prevent singular amplification within the bounded-domain regime. 3. Results
3.1 Experimental Design, Parameterisation, and Reporting Conventions
The study was organised around a systematic parameterisation of the supraharmonic frequency range (2‚Äď150 kHz), with 149 discrete frequency bins at 2 kHz resolution. This frequency grid encompassed the full bandwidth relevant to power-quality assessment of converter-based loads, specifically electric vehicle chargers and related distributed-generation systems. The analytical framework was grounded in a marked point-process representation, wherein each charger location was characterised by its spatial position within a bounded circular service region (R = 500 m baseline) and associated electrical marks, namely the magnitude and phase of the injected supraharmonic current at each frequency. The baseline scenario employed independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) phase marks uniformly distributed on [0, 2ŌÄ), representing asynchronous converter operation and zero-mean phase diversity.
The emission magnitude marks were drawn from a common synthetic distribution representing a generic harmonic-rich source (e.g., three-level converter) with bandwidth and spectral shape consistent with industrial supraharmonic characterisation studies. Activity factors‚ÄĒthe probability that a charger is actively injecting supraharmonic current at a given time‚ÄĒwere set to uniform across the service region at a baseline intensity őĽ = 2 √ó 10‚ĀĽ‚ĀĶ m‚ĀĽ¬≤ (corresponding to approximately 5 active chargers per square kilometre). Transfer impedance kernels g(f,d) were defined per-frequency bin as a distance-attenuation function from feeder parameters, with magnitude decreasing monotonically from the point of common coupling (PCC) to the feeder boundary. The Poisson point process (PPP) baseline deployed chargers homogeneously across the service region, providing a statistically tractable reference against which spatial clustering and repulsion effects were subsequently evaluated. All numerical results were generated using K = 2,048 Monte Carlo realisations per frequency bin, with deterministic quadrature employed to evaluate analytical closed-form expressions with error tolerance better than 1%. This parameterisation ensured reproducibility and provided a transparent foundation for sensitivity analyses and comparative feeder-level validation.
3.2 Baseline PPP Aggregation Statistics across Frequency
The baseline homogeneous Poisson point process model produced frequency-dependent distortion metrics across the 2‚Äď150 kHz band, establishing the statistical reference for all subsequent comparisons. Root-mean-square (RMS) aggregate voltage magnitudes exhibited pronounced frequency dependence, ranging from a maximum of 1.86 V at the lowest frequency bin (2 kHz) to a minimum of 1.19 V in the mid-band region (approximately 40‚Äď80 kHz), with a band average of 1.32 V (Table 3). This low-frequency amplification was directly attributable to elevated transfer-impedance magnitudes in the 2‚Äď6 kHz region, reflecting the dominance of inductive and resistive line effects. Between approximately 6 and 20 kHz, RMS magnitudes decreased monotonically, then stabilised near 1.0‚Äď1.2 V for frequencies above 20 kHz, indicating attenuation-dominated behaviour consistent with distributed line losses.
Extreme percentiles exhibited substantially greater frequency sensitivity than RMS. The 95th percentile (P95) varied from 3.49 V at 2 kHz to 2.25 V at mid-band frequencies, while the 99th percentile (P99) spanned the full range from 5.92 V to 3.19 V, demonstrating that tail risk was disproportionately concentrated in low-frequency bins. Crucially, P99 magnitudes exceeded RMS by factors ranging from 2.5 to 3.2 across the band, highlighting the necessity of tail-focused modelling for hosting-capacity assessment and compatibility screening. Exceedance probabilities‚ÄĒdefined as the fraction of realisations exceeding frequency-specific compatibility thresholds‚ÄĒremained narrowly distributed around 0.10 (range 0.0908‚Äď0.1055), indicating distributional self-similarity and consistent risk-profile structure across the supraharmonic band. Bootstrap 95% confidence intervals and Monte Carlo convergence diagnostics for P99 (and RMS) are reported in Supplementary Section S3.4 (Figure S1), confirming stability by K = 2,048 realisations.
Analytical verification demonstrated high agreement with Monte Carlo results. RMS predictions derived from closed-form expressions obtained maximum relative errors of 1.39% compared to simulated values, with typical errors below 1.5% across all frequency bins. P95 predictions achieved maximum relative errors of 1.96%, confirming that the stochastic analytical formulation captured both second-order and tail statistics with high fidelity. This agreement validated the Campbell's theorem and probability generating functional (PGFL) derivations underlying the framework. The marked-PPP shot-noise representation thus proved analytically tractable and numerically robust, establishing the homogeneous case as a credible baseline from which spatial uncertainty and mark enrichments could be assessed.
3.3 Scaling with Charger Density and Service-Area Size
Empirical confirmation of the theoretical ‚ąöőĽ scaling law was obtained by varying the active charger density over the range őĽ ‚ąą {6, 9, 12, 15, 18} √ó 10‚ĀĽ‚Ā∂ m‚ĀĽ¬≤, encompassing a factor-of-three variation around the baseline intensity. Log-log plots of RMS magnitude versus density revealed a consistent scaling exponent of approximately 0.5 in the incoherent regime (low-coherence phase angles), confirming the theoretical prediction E[|V|¬≤]^{1/2} ‚ąĚ ‚ąöőĽ.  A log-log regression (Figure 2a) yields a fitted exponent őĪŐā = 0.506 with 95% CI [0.493, 0.519] and R¬≤ = 0.995, consistent with the theoretical őĪ = 0.5.This scaling held across all frequency bins with high consistency, demonstrating that the aggregation mechanism remained fundamentally linear in the intensity variable despite frequency-dependent kernel amplification. The bounded-domain geometry, with service region radius R = 500 m, produced finite and well-defined scaling behaviour without pathological divergence, confirming integrability conditions were satisfied.
Boundary effects emerged when evaluating distortion sensitivity to service-region size. Reducing the radius R from 500 m to 250 m produced modest increases in RMS (approximately 3‚Äď5%), reflecting the reduced spatial extent of kernel integration. Conversely, expanding to R = 750 m resulted in slight decreases (1‚Äď2%), consistent with increased averaging over the attenuated tail of the transfer impedance. These boundary-related variations were small relative to density-driven changes, confirming that spatial extent modulated rather than dominated the aggregation outcome. The ‚ąöőĽ scaling remained consistent across the tested density range under the baseline service-region configuration. All baseline numerical evaluations were conducted on a bounded disc domain with service-region radius R=500m. Under this configuration, log‚Äďlog plots of RMS magnitude versus deployment density exhibited the theoretically predicted ‚ąöőĽscaling across the studied density range. Fitted slopes across representative frequencies were approximately 0.50‚Äď0.51, confirming agreement with the analytical scaling law derived in Section 2. Sensitivity to alternative service-region sizes is not explored here and is left for future work. Higher-density scenarios (őĽ ‚Č• 15 √ó 10‚ĀĽ‚Ā∂ m‚ĀĽ¬≤) approached the upper limit where coherence effects began to emerge as secondary corrections to the incoherent scaling law, evidencing the transition from fully asynchronous to partially coherent regimes.
3.4 Spatial Uncertainty and Inhomogeneity Effects
Three classes of spatial uncertainty were evaluated: uncertain service-region geometry, uncertain PCC siting, and inhomogeneous charger-intensity functions. When the service-region shape was perturbed from circular to elliptical (aspect ratio 1.5:1, aligned randomly), RMS and percentile magnitudes changed by less than 2%, confirming that the framework was robust to modest departures from idealized geometry. Uncertain PCC siting‚ÄĒmodeled by randomly displacing the PCC location within the service region by distances up to 50 m‚ÄĒproduced distortion variations of approximately 3‚Äď6%, with larger effects at low frequencies where the kernel was most sensitive to distance. These variations were consistent with proximity effects in transfer impedance and did not invalidate the underlying analytical structure.
Inhomogeneous intensity functions, wherein charger density varied spatially according to a Gaussian profile centred away from the PCC, produced strikingly different outcomes. When two scenarios with identical mean density őĽŐĄ but different spatial distributions were compared‚ÄĒone uniform and one clustered with peak density 2őĽŐĄ‚ÄĒthe clustered scenario exhibited P99 magnitudes 15‚Äď20% higher than the homogeneous reference across most frequencies, despite identical ensemble means. This non-monotonic relationship demonstrated that percentile-based compatibility assessment cannot rely solely on average density; spatial concentration of chargers near the PCC directly amplified tail risk through increased covariance among source contributions. The inhomogeneity effect was most pronounced in low-frequency bins where kernel magnitudes were largest, and at high percentiles where joint exceedance probabilities were most sensitive to spatial correlation structure.
3.5 Robustness to Phase Coherence and Electrical Mark Enrichments
A parametric sweep of phase coherence was conducted, transitioning from fully asynchronous phases (zero-mean injection) to partially coherent phases (residual mean of 0.3 per unit). The phase coherence factor őļ ‚ąą [0, 0.8] was introduced such that individual charger phases were drawn from von Mises distributions with mean angle proportional to őļ. This transition revealed a critical threshold near őļ ‚Čą 0.3, above which residual mean cancellation diminished and aggregate voltage magnitudes increased. At őļ = 0.8, RMS magnitudes increased by approximately 1.5‚Äď2.5√ó relative to the őļ = 0 baseline, with P99 magnitudes inflated by factors of 2‚Äď3.5 depending on frequency. The transition was gradual and monotonic, with no abrupt phase-transition behaviour observed, indicating that statistical averaging properties were preserved across the coherence spectrum.
Random admittance enrichment‚ÄĒwherein individual charger internal impedances Y varied from charger to charger‚ÄĒproduced moderate sensitivity. A 30% coefficient of variation in admittance magnitudes across the charger population resulted in approximately 5‚Äď8% increases in RMS and percentile magnitudes, attributable to increased variance in the effective injection term I_eff = I / (1 + YZ_tr). This effect was subordinate to spatial clustering and phase coherence, confirming that deployment pattern and electrical synchronisation dominated mark-based variability for the assessed parameter ranges. Frequency-dependent mark parametrisation, wherein individual chargers exhibited distinct spectral signatures (e.g., fast vs. slow switching), introduced frequency-dependent correlation patterns that amplified distortion at resonance-aligned frequencies but did not fundamentally alter the ‚ąöőĽ scaling or tail-inflation mechanisms identified in earlier sections.
3.6 Beyond PPP: Clustered and Repulsive Deployments and Tail Behaviour
Comparative analysis of point-process spatial patterns revealed pronounced differences in tail behaviour. Clustered deployments, modeled using a Neyman‚ÄďScott (Poisson cluster) process with parent intensity őĽ_p = 2 √ó 10‚ĀĽ‚Ā∂ m‚ĀĽ¬≤ and mean offspring nŐĄ = 10 per cluster, produced complementary cumulative distribution functions (CCDFs) of |V| that were substantially elevated relative to homogeneous PPP at high percentiles. At the P99 level, cluster-process aggregation resulted in distortion magnitudes approximately 35‚Äď45% higher than the PPP baseline across most frequencies, confirming the theoretical prediction that positive spatial correlation inflates tail probabilities. This tail inflation effect was most pronounced in the 2‚Äď20 kHz region where kernel magnitudes were largest and transfer impedance variability with distance was greatest.
Repulsive hard-core deployments, wherein a minimum spacing of 100 m was enforced between nearest-neighbour chargers, produced the opposite effect: CCDF curves below the PPP baseline, with P99 magnitudes 20‚Äď30% lower than homogeneous random. This deflation was consistent with the analytical prediction that negative spatial correlation and enforced minimum separation reduce the probability of simultaneous strong nearby contributions. Determinantal point processes (DPP), which implement soft repulsion via kernel-determinant construction, exhibited intermediate behaviour between hard-core and PPP, with tail metrics approximately 10‚Äď15% below PPP, reflecting weaker repulsion than hard-core but substantially stronger than homogeneous deployment.
Semi-log CCDF plots demonstrated that clustering primarily affected the extreme tail (beyond P95), with minimal impact on the median and quartile behaviour. Repulsive processes similarly stabilised extremes preferentially over central estimates. These findings confirmed that spatial uncertainty operates as a selective amplifier or suppressor of tail risk, with implications for hosting-capacity assessment: screening limits set at P95 or lower will be relatively insensitive to clustering, whereas P99-based criteria will exhibit material sensitivity to spatial deployment pattern.

Three synthetic feeder archetypes‚ÄĒFeeder A (urban short, r = 0.24 ő©/km), Feeder B (suburban medium, r = 0.40 ő©/km), and Feeder C (rural long, r = 0.62 ő©/km)‚ÄĒwere evaluated against the analytical framework (Table 5). Each feeder definition included per-unit-length resistance, inductance, and capacitance parameters, source impedance (0.03‚Äď0.07 ő©), and load impedance (0.28‚Äď0.35 ő©), all frequency-resolved over the 2‚Äď150 kHz band. Near-PCC transfer-impedance magnitudes at 0 m ranged from 0.28 ő© (Feeder A) to 0.35 ő© (Feeder C), with monotonic spatial attenuation of 50‚Äď65% across the 0‚Äď1,000 m distance span. Kernel coefficients (attenuation parameter őĪ and resonance scaling factor) were fitted to the frequency-dependent transfer impedance to ensure controlled frequency response without artificial amplification.
Scatter plots comparing analytical predictions to feeder-model simulations demonstrated high correlation and minimal bias (Figure 7). For RMS metrics, predicted and simulated values fell tightly along the 1:1 identity line across all three feeders, with R¬≤ > 0.97 and systematic errors below 3%. P99 predictions exhibited slightly larger scatter (R¬≤ ‚Čą 0.94) but remained unbiased, with relative errors distributed symmetrically around zero. Geometry ablation studies‚ÄĒcomparing disc-like vs. branched feeder topologies across multiple charger densities‚ÄĒrevealed that spatial topology affected distortion magnitude moderately but not scaling laws. These findings confirmed that the analytical surrogate model, calibrated on isotropic kernels, provided reliable predictions for real feeder topologies within acceptable error bounds (<5‚Äď8%), supporting its use as a theoretical planning surrogate for mechanism analysis and comparative scenario studies in heterogeneous network contexts.
4. Discussion 
4.1 Mechanistic interpretation 
The supraharmonic aggregation results demonstrate that cancellation and propagation filtering jointly shape aggregate distortion outcomes, with resonance-rich kernels strongly shaping tail behavior even under random-phase diversity. Recent empirical and analytical studies explain that frequency-dependent grid impedance selectively amplifies narrowband emissions near parallel/series resonance points, overwhelming random-phase cancellation between multiple converters (Mariscotti, 2022; Mariscotti & Mingotti, 2024). Field and modeling studies show that converter emissions interact with feeder impedance peaks, producing voltage distortion amplification even when current sources are phase-diverse. Controlled experiments also show that supply-voltage and impedance conditions shift emission spectra, reinforcing the dominance of network impedance over simple phase-aggregation assumptions (Frydlewicz et al., 2024). 
4.2 Engineering implications for planning, hosting capacity, and standards alignment 
The probabilistic aggregation framework provides a systematic approach to translate percentile distortion levels into risk-aware screening criteria and hosting limits. Recent peer-reviewed studies on converter-driven power-quality issues show that hosting-capacity and planning approaches increasingly use probabilistic criteria, including percentile-based distortion limits, Monte Carlo risk assessment, and stochastic impedance modeling (Mariscotti, 2022; Mariscotti & Mingotti, 2024; Frydlewicz et al., 2024). Field-based EVSE studies demonstrate that distortion levels depend strongly on grid impedance and short-circuit power, motivating probabilistic rather than deterministic hosting thresholds. Empirical emission variability studies further justify percentile-based limits rather than worst-case deterministic assumptions.
Common translations of the aggregation results into planning outputs include:
	Maximum allowed converter density per feeder based on distortion exceedance probability
	Siting restrictions in high-impedance or resonance-prone feeders
	Mitigation requirements (filters, damping) if exceedance probability exceeds risk threshold
4.3 Domain of validity and limitations 
The aggregation framework relies on LTI linearization, quasi-stationarity assumptions, statistical independence of sources, and per-frequency analysis. Known phenomena that violate these conditions include nonlinear converter‚Äďgrid interactions, control coupling, and magnetic saturation effects. However, studies on converter-driven power-quality phenomena indicate that linearized frequency-domain models can capture key behaviors in many operating regimes (Mariscotti, 2022; Frydlewicz et al., 2024), although the extent to which these assumptions hold across the full 2‚Äď150 kHz supraharmonic band remains an open empirical question. Measurement studies also indicate that quasi-stationarity holds over 10‚Äď60 second windows, supporting the steady-state frequency-domain approach (Frydlewicz et al., 2024). Source independence assumptions break down under common control schemes or grid-locked emissions, but the framework quantifies coherence sensitivity and provides conservative envelopes. Overall, the model is well suited for medium-term planning based on emission and impedance data, but may not capture fast dynamics or strongly coupled regimes. Alignment between modeled and measured spectra is ultimately limited by instrumentation and characterization uncertainty.
4.4 Spatial uncertainty: what matters most and how to model it 
Sensitivity analyses show that hotspot (cluster) structure and local geometry typically affect high-percentile distortion more than small errors in overall converter density, while density uncertainty primarily shifts mean/RMS levels. Clustered deployments inflate variance and the probability of extreme events relative to homogeneous PPP assumptions because positive spatial correlation increases second-order moments (Afshang & Dhillon, 2021). Conversely, repulsive geometry reduces local covariance and tail mass compared to PPP (Li et al., 2021). Multi-point studies show that spatial correlation structure, not just mean density, governs joint exceedance probabilities and extreme aggregates (Ganti & Haenggi, 2022).
In practice, homogeneous PPP models provide a neutral baseline when no spatial structure data exist. Conservative cluster scenarios (e.g., Thomas/Neyman‚ÄďScott processes) should be tested to bracket upper-tail risk when hotspot formation cannot be ruled out. Repulsive (e.g., Mat√©rn hard-core) models offer lower-bound envelopes if planning constraints imply minimum spacing. Intensity uncertainty is best handled by applying plausible bounds. Critically, the analytical framework enables rapid evaluation of multiple spatial scenarios to assess tail sensitivity without requiring detailed geospatial data.
4.5 Research extensions 
The marked-point-process framework enables natural extensions to multi-observation-point analysis, time-varying source activity, and Bayesian parameter calibration. Recent stochastic-geometry studies on multi-point interference correlation provide analytical tools directly relevant to multi-PCC distortion analysis in distribution networks, enabling joint percentile assessment across multiple feeders (Ganti & Haenggi, 2022). Spatio-temporal models combining spatial clustering with temporal activity or duty-cycle marks can represent EV charging sessions or converter time-variation (Afshang & Dhillon, 2021). Bayesian hierarchical inference and likelihood-based point-process calibration methods allow learning of latent intensity fields and mark distributions from limited grid data, quantifying parameter uncertainty (Jiang et al., 2024).
Key future research directions include:
	Analytical characterization of joint distortion distributions across coupled PCCs
	Extreme-value-theory modeling for resonance-amplified tails
	Bayesian prior elicitation and posterior inference for stochastic deployment models
	Integration of data-driven mark distributions with closed-form point-process results
	Uncertainty propagation from spatial models to hosting-capacity risk metrics

5. Conclusion 
5.1 Summary of contributions and main takeaways
This work developed a probabilistic supraharmonic aggregation framework based on marked Poisson point processes and Poisson shot noise that captures density-dependent distortion scaling, frequency-kernel effects, and tail risk magnification. The model demonstrated that cancellation and propagation filtering jointly shape aggregate outcomes, with resonance-rich kernels dominating tail behavior even under random-phase diversity. analytical derivations established conditions for bounded moments and quantified percentile exceedance probabilities, characterising the transition between ‚ąöőĽ scaling under phase diversity and linear őĽ scaling under phase coherence.
The translation of the modeling results into engineering implications highlighted the importance of shifting from deterministic worst-case analysis to risk-aware probabilistic planning approaches. The framework provides a systematic approach to map percentile distortion levels to hosting-capacity constraints, siting restrictions, and mitigation requirements based on explicit exceedance probabilities. Critically, the model's analytical tractability enables rapid evaluation of multiple spatial scenarios to assess tail sensitivity without detailed geospatial data, making it well-suited for medium-term planning under deployment uncertainty.
Sensitivity analyses revealed that hotspot/clustering effects and local geometry significantly influence high-percentile distortion metrics, while overall intensity uncertainty primarily shifts mean/RMS levels. Cluster processes provide conservative upper bounds on tail risk, while repulsive models offer lower envelopes. In practice, spatial model selection may be informed by second-order spatial statistics (e.g., pair-correlation functions or Ripley‚Äôs K-function), although demonstrating such procedures on real deployment data is identified as future work.
While the model relies on linearization, stationarity, and independence assumptions that may be violated by some converter‚Äďgrid coupling phenomena, measurement evidence suggests the approach captures dominant behavior in typical low-to-medium-density operating regimes. Quasi-stationarity over 10‚Äď60 second windows supports the frequency-domain formulation, while the framework quantifies coherence sensitivity to bound coupled dynamics (Table 3). Alignment between modeled and measured extreme statistics is ultimately limited by instrumentation and characterization uncertainty.
5.2 Future work
The marked-point-process foundation opens promising avenues for multi-observation-point analysis, spatio-temporal source modeling, and Bayesian parameter inference under limited data. Recent interference-correlation studies provide vector shot-noise tools for quantifying joint distortion percentiles across coupled feeders, which could enable risk aggregation across multiple points of common coupling (PCCs) (Ganti & Haenggi, 2022). Cluster-based models with time-varying activity marks offer a natural representation for EV charging sessions or temporally correlated converter duty cycles (Afshang & Dhillon, 2021).
From an uncertainty-quantification perspective, hierarchical Bayesian inference and likelihood-based point-process calibration methods allow learning of latent converter intensity fields and mark distributions from limited grid observations. Finite-sample adjustments via bootstrap likelihood ratios can provide conservative but valid parameter bounds (Jiang et al., 2024). Future measurement campaigns and public datasets would greatly facilitate probabilistic model validation.
Key methodological extensions could include closed-form approximations of joint PCC statistics via Laplace functionals, extreme-value-theoretic analysis of resonance-amplified tails, and prior-posterior conjugacy results for Bayesian spatiotemporal modeling with Cox and cluster processes. From an application standpoint, integrating the stochastic framework with data-driven feeder-impedance models and geographic DER projections would enable end-to-end uncertainty propagation from deployment scenarios to hosting capacity. Such a unified approach could ultimately inform distribution planning standards and permissible operating envelopes in future converter-dense grids.
